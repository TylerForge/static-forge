Data Viz Case Study: Utility Billing

Kaila outlines her process for solving a data viz problem for a UB team here at Tyler. 

The original

This is the original. The team knew that this was a lot of information, displayed in a way that was hard to decipher. So, they asked me to provide some information about what I would change and why. Here is how I walked through this process with them.

 

Getting started

1. To start thinking visually, I first consider two questions about the nature and purpose of the visualization:

       Is the information conceptual or data driven?

       Am I declaring something or exploring something?

 

In this case, the information is data driven, and the product team was declaring something.

 

2. Then, I determine the “why.” What is the purpose of this chart? What are you convincing users of? If, after seeing this chart, a user could walk away with one piece of information, what would that be?

Most users are at this chart because they want to know “why is my utility bill so high/low?” So, this chart should display the answer.

The team did some research with users and found out:

“The chart is needed for citizens but will mostly be used by the clerks when answering questions the citizens may have. I can see them referencing the chart during calls to help answer questions.”

I asked lots of follow up questions: what were your findings? What does the user typically want to compare? If I were scanning this chart in a few seconds, what would be the one takeaway I should have? It sounds like the answer to this will vary based on whether you are a clerk or a citizen: can we define the impact it should have on both of those personas?

My recommendation:

I recommended representing this data as a comparison graph: showing last year’s bill and this year’s bill together.

I told the team:

“I think the best thing you can do for users is help them answer the question ‘why is my bill low/high’ –any system-provided suggestions will be helpful here (e.g. your area has been over the average temperature this month, comparisons to national/local data, their own previous consumption). I would also use this as an opportunity to give them actionable feedback via suggestions to lower their consumption (e.g. here are 5 things you can do to lower your bill, or “your area is usually 20 degrees hotter next month. Here’s what you can do to prepare.”) 

I also suggested looking outside the bounds of this chart at the overall user experience. If we expect that our target user is going to come into our software and find this chart, then we may miss the mark. Instead—we need to find our users. Should we be sending them notifications due to this bill oddity and not relying on them to come to us?

 

Probably.

 

So, that’s what we did.

 

I found in testing that this update should provide (for bill oddities): the amount of difference in the bill since the last month’s bill (cost and consumption), in addition to the amount they paid for that month the previous year. If possible the system should provide informed suggestions for why the oddity happened.

Then: if the user then needs additional information, a link is provided to this graph. 

Here’s what I changed:

  • Chart is now month view by default (user research suggested that this was the most valuable view for our clients).

  • I cleaned up the unnecessary: extra axis labels, decimal points, etc.

  • Used a line chart to display the data for utility consumption over time instead of a bar.

  • This graph is showing consumption, but it defaults to showing cost. It’s possible to toggle between the two by changing the word in the title (cost/consumption) via the dropdown.

  • Options for turning on/off the data (temp, rainfall, last year, this year): I’d say just the last year/this year data is turned on by default.

  • Use green to represent cost. Last year and this year’s data belong to the same category of variable (only difference is time) so they are the same color but different hues. Last year is a lighter hue so that this year stands out and draws attention.

  • Buttons on bottom of card allow you to take necessary actions–troubleshoot the reason for your bill being high, or manage alerts (other action possibilities could go here or in an overflow menu/vertical ellipse menu at the top right of the card).

  • Colors pass colorblind/ADA compliance tests

  • Put labels next to their respective data points, instead of in a key. This allows for easy scannability and quickens the user’s consumption of the information

     

Alternate year view of the chart, with all available data shown:

  • I took out the areal graph for temperature because it carried heavy visual emphasis. It showed more minute data, but is red and draws the eye, which distracts from the overall point of the chart–which is utility history. Summarizing the temperature with bar graphs makes more sense to me.
  • I’ve used bar graphs for the temperature and rainfall because we are displaying averages–which I think limits the data to only what the user needs to know. The utility data is not an average, and has more refined spikes and curves that you want to call attention to in comparison with last year’s data, which is why we want to display that in a line graph.
  • I’ve grouped these bars together instead of putting equal distance between all the bars, so that it is clear which bars belong to which months.
  • Using blue for rainfall, red for temperature because both of those are common expectation and it strengthens the visual.
  • Checkboxes allow you to toggle on and off the data but also act as a key–so you do not need to duplicate that information.
