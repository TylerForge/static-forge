1. Introduction

This article is primarily targeted for product owners/managers interested in product analytics and uses in informal style for terminology and concepts. Given this, this article is not suitable for UX researchers looking for formal training. There is useful information for UX researchers, and engineers looking to implement tooling. This article is also primarily targeted for web applications, but core concepts apply to applications on other platforms.

1.1 What is product analytics?

Product analytics, often referred to as big data analytics, is a form of telemetry (greek roots tele, “remote”, and metron “measure”, or the act of remote observation) which involves the automated collection of incidental usage data in volume and assists in an intuitive understanding of users and the usage of your product. Product analytics is one type of telemetry, and can coexist with other types of telemetry like marketing, engineering, support, etc. with varying levels of overlaps in data collection. The key characteristics of product analytics (if collected properly) are:

  • Independence – data is not influenced by other teams in your organization
  • Directness – data is collected directly from actual users of your product
  • Evidences value – data collected and resulting analysis can be considered reliable referential data 

Customer engagement complements product analytics by actively interacting with your users and collecting direct feedback using tools like surveys, polls, onboarding, and A/B testing.

1.2 Why?

Product development is an expensive endeavor. To quote Energov, a typical 6-developer team and their associated QA, DevOps, Product management, Delivery, etc. costs Tyler about $1.08 million a year (in 2019). This is likely a conservative estimate with several Tyler teams easily exceeding this amount. When making an investment of this magnitude, it is imperative that released products maximize revenue generation.

The current marketplace is highly competitive and agile. In this paradigm, direct end user feedback drives the vast majority of short- and near-term development activity. Every insight you can gain from observing how end users use your product and what they really want can go a long way in giving you that competitive edge.

Tyler, as a company, has been very successful in the past roughly two decades using legacy platforms and delivering large volumes of features without much insight into how users use their products (or not). With the industry moving towards intuitive cloud solutions, Tyler will no longer be able to rely on its successful approach of the past two decades into the future. We can choose to evolve or be left behind.

Product analytics is a key tool that is able to provide insights to both optimize our products for actual users, and maximize revenues and user retention. Successful product analytics implementations require long term commitment to systematic collection of telemetry. It has an element of science in it in terms of tooling and best practices, and an element of art in it in terms of knowing what questions to ask and how to capture it.

1.3 Who is it for?

Anyone who is involved with product design and development, including product owners, UX designers/researchers, and engineers. However, it is a critical tool for Product owners/managers because their priorities are often driven by sales, marketing, engineering, support, and executive and they are rarely able to challenge or balance those demands due to lack of independent data. Product analytics can provide that vital information to balance these disparate priorities.

1.5 What are the limitations of product analytics?

Product analytics is a measure of the current and historical usage patterns of your product. They can measure the effectiveness of your product and can give indications of what your users are looking for that are missing. Combined with customer engagement tools, you could also glean what your user desires are. However, product analytics cannot be used as a standalone tool for long term product strategy as the collected data is still effectively limited by the current product environment. Long term strategy requires deeper market and trend analysis the data for which your product analytics telemetry may not contain.

Like most things computing, product analytics is also subject to garbage-in, garbage-out. A frequent mistake made by product managers/owners is to hook their product into a popular product analytics tool and expect it to magically answer all their questions. Instead, they end up being immediately inundated with large volumes of data that either make no sense or unreliable to make critical product decisions. At best they end up using portions of the data like browser/device types, peak usage hours, etc. and discard the rest. To reemphasize a point made earlier, product analytics requires a disciplined, long-term, and business-value based data collection and analysis. Quality of data should be prioritized over quantity of data. Ask simple questions first before tooling more complex questions.

2. Core concepts

2.1 Difference between marketing (page) and product (event) analytics

The first introduction to product analytics for most product owners/managers is typically through exposure to google analytics, as it tends to be heavily used for marketing use. This begs the question as what the difference between marketing and product analytics since there is significant overlap between the two types. It boils down to “page” tracking vs. “event” tracking:

  • Marketing use of usage data is primary towards understanding where users come from, where they go to, how many of them successfully complete revenue generating activity, and how successful the overall product is at retaining customers. Essentially, marketing analytics focuses on origins and “page views”. Page views can provide the following information depending on the solution used:
    • User demographics (location, age, sex, etc.)
    • Referrals (which sources, including social media like facebook, etc. did the user come from)
    • Pages, the order of pages viewed, and drop offs
    • Goals based on pages visited
  • Product analytics is focused on feature effectiveness by collecting what the user is doing. Essentially, product analytics is about “key events”. By tracking events instead of pages, product analytics has the advantages of (a) capturing activity within a page, and (b) avoid noise in data by jumping over pages not relevant to the analysis. Events can provide the following information:
    • What did the user do, esp. within a page
    • Event flows in order, along with drop offs
    • Goals based on events

For functionality that is spread out over multiple pages with each page containing just one activity, both marketing and product analytics can leverage the same data. However, modern web applications typically adopt the Single Page Application (SPA) paradigm which makes it hard to realize this in reality except for the simplest of applications.

2.2 Cohorts or segments

In the context of product analytics, cohorts or segments refers to a group of people with specific characteristics that you are attempting to analyze. It could be a group of people who come from a certain geography, use a specific type of browser or device, perform a certain activity, or a combination of several of these. Typically, cohorts or segments are defined using attribute filters, where attributes are typically collected by the product analytics tooling by default.

2.3 Goals

Goals are typically used to measure success rate of a desirable activity, and the extent to which that progression occurs. Typically goals have two or more pages or events that define the progression towards the goals.

2.4 Analysis aids

Telemetry can be presented in a few formats:

  • Flows or Funnels: These present the navigation histogram of users based on pages or events the users selected through their journeys through your product. The various branching out and drop offs at each stage is presented.
  • Heatmaps: These present the frequency with which certain areas of a page were access using gradient coloring. Frequently used regions are typically colored red and least used regions expose the page colors.
  • User activity recordings: These present actual user activity by individual users. They can be setup to either hide the user identity or expose it. Given this, use of this should be very limited.

Customer engagement provides addition aids to collect direct feedback:

  • Surveys: These present a set of questions for end users. They can be open to all or be targeted to specific cohorts. They can be automatically presented or something users invoke manually. Questions should typically not take more than 1-2 minutes of the user’s time (for longer formats, it is better to give the user an option to opt-in and be contacted through a different process). The simpler the surveys the more feedback you will likely to receive.
  • Polls: These present the frequency with which certain areas of a page were access using gradient coloring. Frequently used regions are typically colored red and least used regions expose the page colors.
  • Onboarding: Onboarding allows you to provide introductory information to functionality as users interact with your product. It can be a single set of screens presented up on launch, or be targeted to specific areas of functionality.
  • A/B testing: In the context of automated analytics, A/B testing allows the presentation of different sets of experiences to different sets of users and try to compare the relative success rates between the two choices.

3. Getting started

3.1 Documenting your business questions

Before getting into integrating the tooling and collecting telemetry, it is imperative that you have a solid understanding of what you kind of information you need to collect to answer your business question(s). Start with very simple questions to gain experience and then expand as you gain more confidence. The information to be collected are:

  • The question for which you would like the answer(s) for. Keep the question as simple/narrow as possible initially.
  • Business impact of having the question answered.
  • The narrowest E2E or Customer journey for this question.
  • The minimum set of key pages (simple applications) or events (complex applications) that would help answer the question.
  • Your analysis approach to answering the question using the collected telemetry.
  • Once you have documented your business question in detail, you can prepare them for implementation by giving them GA tags. GA allows a total of 4 hierarchical tags (Category, Action, Label, Value) to be applied to an event. The first 3 are pure labels with just the parent-child-grand child hierarchy and carry no special meaning to GA other than the hierarchy. The 4th tag is numerical only and is only used when cumulative totals or averages are required – unfortunately, it does not allow for histogram results. Based on this, our recommended usage for most simple scenarios are:
    • Category=The customer journey that is being studied. This is a broad value representing your belief of a particular path that a user takes to perform a specific activity.
    • Action=This is the particular study or area being researched.
    • Label=This is event (button click, etc.) within that area of study
    • Value=Blank in most cases
Have your document reviewed by your stakeholders as needed. Use the Excel template below to get an idea of how to document this information in a formal manner.

4. Product analytics tooling

4.1 Overview

Doe to the complexity of tooling, this article only covers the discussion for web applications. It does not cover tooling considerations for smartphone apps and desktop applications, though some tooling options discussed here also offer extensions into these platforms.

A key criteria for evaluating product analytic tooling is the degree to which it relies on engineering to made code changes to the product to answer specific questions. All product analytics tooling require a small bit of standard code on a UI page that is accessed by end users. Ideally, that code is included in a UI template that automatically injects it on every UI page. Ideal product analytics tools are then able to use that code to essentially transfer control over to product owners/managers to instrument measurements as they see fit to answer their questions.

A second criteria for evaluation is how much of an expertise is expected from product owners/managers. Some tools are highly visual and therefore suitable for people with no html knowledge. Others require a modicum of html knowledge like knowing how to find element ids.

A third criteria for evaluation is your require customer engagement tools to solicit direct feedback.

4.2 Review of tools

The CorpDev team has reviewed some of these tools along with the D&I division which is currently implementing Pendo into their web applications.

Our recommendation for most people wanting to explore product analytics is to consider the Google’s Universal Analytics (analytical engine, “GA”) + Tag Manager (data pipeline, “GTM”) combination for the following reasons:

  • “Free as in beer”: hosted and ready to be used (free tier includes 10 million data points/month) requiring the least amount of engineering bandwidth for continuously available long term use. Compared to this, commercial solutions after a trial period have costs start around mid-5 figures all the way to 6 figures, and is typically charged per active user on your application. Matomo cloud does offer a hosting only option in the thousands, but that is still money if you are not willing to host the open source solution yourself.
  • Powerful tools and capabilities for core product analytics needs
  • Used industry wide
  • Extremely flexible; GTM allows you to hook multiple analytics solutions all at once and you can add paid solutions later. It also allows product owners to define events with basic HTML knowledge, and provides full access management to provide read or edit permissions.

Negatives:

  • Need to trust Google.
  • GA uses data sampling for its free tier, which can reduce the accuracy of analysis the larger the dataset grows.
  • Interfaces are overloaded and inconsistent terminology between the two tools can easily confuse a new user (hopefully this article will help with issue).
  • Requires very basic HTML knowledge (willingness to learn a few concepts)
  • Google Tag Manger can become a security concern if account access is compromised (must enable 2-Factor authentication to a smart phone if using it)
  • Does not include customer engagement tools

Comparison: 

Once you have establish a baseline expertise with product analytics, and either need functionality beyond what GA-GTM offers or need more ease of use, you may consider very comparable open source solution matomo or any commercial solution like Pendo, Heap.io, Amplitude, hotjar, WalkMe Insights, etc. Pendo is currently the most feature rich and easy to use commercial solution, and has been adopted by the D&I group.

5. GA+GTM solution

This section assumes that you have reviewed and completed the excel template in Section 3, to identify and define a very simple business question and have decided the events to instrument to answer the question. You should not target more than 2-3 events for your first attempt.

As outlined under 4.2, GA+GTM solution is a quick way to get familiar with product analytics without requiring a big budget or significant development time. This article provides guidance on implementing this particular solution. Both GA and GTM are independent google offerings and can be used independently.

Google Tag Manger is the data pipeline, i.e., GTM javascript code is the one that is included on all web applications and collects data on behalf of all analytical engines. It does not have any analytical capability of its own, but it allows defining events and tracking both pages and events for any analytical tool that needs it. Being from the same company, GTM couples very tightly with Google Universal Analytics but works well with other analytical tools, some of which (like Hotjar) actually have a plugin in GTM to easily connect the two solutions. GTM also allows multiple analytical engines at the same time, but the more analytical engines are hooked to your web application, the slower it will load. We recommend no more than 2 analytical engines to be hooked through GTM at any time due to these performance concerns.

Google Universal Analytics is google’s analytical tool to collect and present information through visual aids. Technically, you could use just GA directly instead of using GTM, but GTM has a distinct advantage when it comes to events in that events can be defined by a product owner within GTM, while GA requires engineers to specifically code events throughout the code as product owners request, which makes standalone GA heavily dependent on engineering bandwidth, something we strongly recommend avoiding as a key criteria for tool selection.

An open source source alternative matomo and its corresponding matomo tag manager is very comparable to google universal analytics and google tag manager, and largely uses the same terminology and setup structure. It also includes heatmaps to boot. However, the open source code has to be installed in your environment or you have to sign up for their cloud trial similar to the trials offered by the various other commercial solutions. The UI is not as polished as the google solutions at the time of writing this article. Given these limitations, we continue to consider the GA+GTM combination as the best playground for product managers/owners to learn product analytics.

Note: Access management within GA-GTM is out of scope for this article but you can provide read only or modification permissions to others in both GA and GTM.

5.1 Implementation considerations
  • Given considerable privacy concerns, always solicit the customer’s permission to collect telemetry from their users (“Opt-in model”).
  • Include an options in your administrative console to turn off global script inclusion should a security need arise to turn it all off.
  • Avoid collecting data from input boxes as end users could accidentally entering sensitive or personally identifiable information. Even accidentally collecting such information (e.g. user entering credit card in a search box) could subject the entire data set to additional regulations.
  • Present a GDPR notice for the use of cookies. (Read) 
5.2 Implementation types

The nature of your web application impacts how you instrument the tooling.

  • Multi-tenant web application (e.g. Tyler Cloud Platform products): These web application types are very well suited for collecting comprehensive product analytics across all tenants and products on the platform. 
  • On-prem/SaaS-hosted web applications (e.g. Tyler Hosting): These web application types are constrained to selectively choosing specific customer installations to hook product analytics into and collecting data from specific products.

5.4 Structures of GA & GTM

A Google Universal Analytics account is structured as follows:

  • Account: Not to be confused with the google account itself, an account within GA can be used to represent an independent entity within the organization. If the google account is intended to be used centrally in the organization, an account could represent each division or group. If the account has been created solely for a specific product with no intention of being used in a larger context, then the account simply represents the product team. Note: The free version of GA only allows for a limited number of accounts.
  • Property: A property is a data set collected through GA. Properties are tied to a specific root domain and so they typically represent an environment. For example, you can have different properties for test and production if they have different root domains. Or each property can represent a specific on-prem customer installation. Each property is given a specific data collection id of the form UA-XXXXXXXXX-X. Note: The free version of GA only allows for a limited number of properties.
  • View: Views are filters applied to the dataset in a property for viewing and analysis. You may construct views to filter on specific segments, or in the case of multi-tenant cloud environments, be used to filter the datasets to specific products and tenants.

A Google Tag Manager account is structured as follows:

  • Account: Similiar to GA, and not to be confused with the google account itself, an account within GTM can be used to represent an independent entity within the organization. If the google account is intended to be used centrally in the organization, an account could represent each division or group. If the account has been created solely for a specific product with no intention of being used in a larger context, then the account simply represents the product team. 
  • Container: Similar in concept to docker containers, a container is a fully independent work space containing the definitions necessary to hook a particular web application to a particular GA property. GTM containers are given a data pipeline id of the form GTM-XXXXXXX. GA-Properties and GTM-Containers must be maintained in a 1:1 association. A container contains the following constructs:
    • Variables: These stored parameters that are used frequently in your definitions and allows for the centralized maintenance of values.
    • Triggers: These are event definitions that hook into specific elements or states of your web application. For example you can define a trigger to be raised when a button is clicked or when a certain element in your page becomes visible.
    • Tags: Tags allow the data collected by GTM to be passed along to analytical engines in form of page data, event data, etc.
  • Versions: Once defined, each container needs to be deployed for it to take effect. A version is a particular checkpoint of a container that you can choose to deploy or not, and review for the changes included in that version.
5.5 Completing the basics

Follow the instructions outlined in this link for GA: Get started with Analytics

Enter your web application’s base domain in the property definition.Once you complete this step, note down your GA property Tracking id. You can retrieve that by going to GA->Admin->Property settings->Basic Settings->Tracking Id. We will be using this value in GTM.

Then follow the instructions outline in this link for GTM: Setup and install Tag Manager

Once you have a default workspace in GTM, you can proceed to define variables, triggers, and tags within your GTM container workspace.

5.6 GTM: Defining variables

From the current workspace navigate to Variables->User-defined variables->New. You will be presented with a new variable dialog. Set the following values:

  • Name = GA Property ID
  •  Click on Variable type card to open up a side menu of available variable options and select Google Analytics Settings.
  • On the Google Analytics Settings options, set Tracking ID to the GA Property Tracking ID that you noted in section 5.5.
  • Let or set Cookie Domain to ‘auto’.
  • Click Save.
Now you can use the ‘GA Property ID’ variable in other parts of the configuration. If you ever need to update the Google Tracking ID value, you can easily reflect the change across the board by simply editing this variable.
5.7 GTM: Capturing page views into GA

From the current workspace navigate to Tags->New:

  • Set Name=GA-All Pages
  • Click on Tag Configuration card->Select Google Analytics: Universal Analytics
  • Track Type=Page View (Default)
  • Google Analytics Settings={{GA Property Id}} variable that you created in the earlier step
  • Click on Triggering card->All Pages (Type=Page View)
  • Click Save

With this you have enabled all pages to be tracked by your GA property.

5.8 GTM: Capturing events into GA

Capturing events using GTM into GA involves two steps: (a) First, define a trigger to capture the event in GTM, and (b) Second, pass the GTM event as a GA event using a Tag.

Defining triggers requires a modicum of HTML knowledge and good code to exist. In particular, you should
get familiar with using Chrome’s F12 option to open the developer tools against your product web page, and use the element inspector to see if you can capture the unique id of your element (if your engineers have coded one). You can also try the element text, etc. but success depends on the underlying element structure. It is best to seek the help of an engineer to create your first triggers and get their advise on how to define these triggers as it largely depends on the type of javascript components your engineers have used in your applications. The current set of TCW components (as of 10/6/2019) unfortunately are not very conducive to be hooked into GTM and so a lot of trial and error should be expected.

From your current workspace navigate to Triggers->New:

  • Name =
  • Click on Trigger Configuration card
  • Under Choose trigger type, there are a variety of options here to hook into your element (like Click) or state of the application (Element visibility). In this example, we will show you how to create a standard Click definition. Choose Click->All Elements
  • Back on the Trigger Configuration card, set “This trigger fires on”=Some Clicks
  • In the option below, you case use Click ID or Text, equals, and either the element id or element text
  • It is additionally good practice to add an additional criteria to limit the definition to a specific application page by using the Page Path, contains, and use the relative path to the application page.
  • Click Save

You have now successfully defined your trigger for your specific button. To create a GA event against this trigger, from your current workspace navigate to Tags->New:

  • Set Name=GA-All Pages
  • Click on Tag Configuration card->Select Google Analytics: Universal Analytics
  • Track Type=Event
  • Category=Value from your Excel sheet as documented in Section 3
  • Action=Value from your Excel sheet as documented in Section 3
  • Label=Value from your Excel sheet as documented in Section 3
  • Google Analytics Settings={{GA Property Id}}
  • Click on Triggering card->Select trigger you created above and return to trigger card
  • Click Save

With this you have now setup GTM to create an event in GA based on your element activity.

5.9 GTM: Development activity - introduce GTM code into product

Now that you have completed the definitions, its time to install Google Tag Manager code into the UI pages of your product (at least the pages you want to track). To do this, from your current workspace click on the GTM key show in the banner of the page adjacent to the Preview and Submit buttons. This should open up the code to be inserted, which is in two parts:

  • Part of code that goes into the section. This code is a must and contains the GTM key included in it.
  • Part of code that goes immediately below the tag. If your application absolutely requires javascript to function, you may be able to skip this code as it is only used if javascript is disabled.

Once engineers have coded this into the UI pages, GTM is now hooked into your product.

5.10 GTM: Previewing setup and publishing container

While you have hooked up your product to GTM and defined your page views and events to be associated with GA, your GTM container is not active because it has not been published yet. Before publishing, you might want to test the integration to make sure you are able to see GTM recognize your page and event tracking. To do this, from your workspace click on ‘Preview’ option in the top right next to the Submit button. This puts the GTM container in preview mode and you should see an orange banner with “Now Previewing Workspace”. Now navigate to the page of your application hooked to the events, and you should see a frame at the bottom that would show all the clicks that GTM is tracking. As you load the page, and click on your defined events, you should see an acknowledgement from GTM for those activities. If you run into issues here, its best to seek the help of an engineer.

Once you have verified that everything looks good in preview mode, select the “Leave Preview Mode” option under the orange banner, and click on Submit. Give a meaningful version name and description, and click on Publish. Your GA-GTM solution is now live and telemetry is being collected.

6. GA Analysis

One data collection has been established successfully, all analysis takes place entirely within Google Universal Analytics. This section deals with the analysis options available within GA. GA has the following sections:

  • Realtime – Shows realtime data (typically past 30 minutes)
  • Audience – Provides page based analysis
  • Acquisition – Search, Ads, Social, Campaigns
  • Behavior – A combination of Page and Event analytics
  • Conversions – Tracking goals

This section will largely focus on Realtime, Audience and Behavior sections

6.1 Realtime data vs. Full data

GA typically takes 24 hours to process telemetry before it becomes available for in-depth analysis due to the volume of data it needs to process across several accounts. However, it present a limited subset of data including page and event activity that has occured in the past 30 minutes. This is helpful for ensuring that data is being collected, but generally not used for actual analysis. To validate that page and event information is being collected can be verfied by looking at the information presented under the following sections:

  • Realtime->Content (pages being accessed)
  • Realtime->Events (events being accessed)

Full analysis is available for data collected later than 24 hours in other sections, particularly Acquisition and Behavior.

6.2 Leveraging GA views

You can leverage GA views functionality, accessible at Admin->Views, to enhance your analysis by creating various views with different filters for obtaining varied pespectives on the same property data. For example you can create views to:

  • Data starting from a particular date
  • Filter on a particular product in a multi-tenant solution
  • Filter on a particular tenant in a multi-tenant solution
  • Filter on specific pages, events, and so on

To create a new view, select Admin->Views->Create View and use the View Settings->Filters to define your filters for the view.

6.3 User or Cohort/Segment based analysis

By default, GA clubs all users under a single “All Users” segment. You can define additional segment definitions for comparison. Segment definitions can be created under Admin->Personal Tools & Assets->Segments.

You can create a new segment by clicking on New Segment. You can use the standard filters like Demographics, Technology, etc. or use the Advanced->Conditions to define cohorts based on specific pages or events. This is a convinient way to filter on users who perform a certain E2E, which is mapped to a customer journey captured in the event Category value.

Give your segment a name and click Save to save the segment. The new segment will now be available in your page and event analysis, particularly under Audience->Cohort Analysis.

6.4 Page based analysis

GA allows for page based analysis in multiple locations:

User forcused page analytics is available under the Audience section:

  • Audience->Demographics: Provide basic user demographics
  • Audience->Geo: Provides user language and location information
  • Audience->Behavior: Provides retention information
  • Audience->Technology & Audience->Mobile: Reflect the platforms used
  • Audience->Users Flow: Provides a funnel view of users accessing pages in order and shows drop offs.

Additional page based analysis is also available at:

  • Behavior->All Pages: Provides a page-centric usage data
  • Behavior->Landing Pages: Provides information on entry points
  • Behavior->Exit Pages: Provides information on exit points
6.5 Event based analysis

Events based flow and drop off rates can be viewed at the following location:

Behavior->Events->Events Flow

The flow can be analyzed further by combining it with the segment definitions to understand the behaviors by specific segments.
