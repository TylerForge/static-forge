Color

The Material Design color system uses an organized approach to applying color to your UI. In this system, a primary and a secondary color are typically selected to represent your brand.

Specifications

Our primary color is Indigo. Our secondary is Amber. We use a third color, Indigo A200, for controls in order to meet WCAG 2.1 compliance standards.

Note: When used against the Tyler background color (Grey 50, #fafafa) or against a white card background, Amber 500 does not pass a sufficient contrast ratio to meet WCAG 2.1 compliance standards. For this reason, controls should use the tertiary color.

Primary color

A primary color is the color displayed most frequently across your app’s screens and components.

Secondary color

A secondary color provides more ways to accent and distinguish your product. A secondary color should be applied sparingly to accent select parts of your UI. The Forge secondary color is applied only to the fab.

Tertiary color

Tyler Forge opted for a tertiary color to provide sufficient ADA contrast for our controls. The tertiary color should be used for the following UI controls:

  • Radio buttons
  • Checkboxes
  • Sliders 
  • Switches
  • Progress indicators

Informational colors

Informational colors typically don’t represent brand, instead they portray extra information to users. In general, informational colors should be used sparingly. For ADA compliance reasons, statuses such as “success,” “warning,” and “error” should not be communicated by color only. Use text in addition to color, or an icon in addition to color to communicate these states. 

  • The success color may be used for communicating workflow status changes, successful actions within an application and other similar patterns. 
  • The warning color may be used for workflow status changes or similar patterns. 
  • The error color indicates invalid data in components, such as invalid text in a text field. It is used in the standard input error styling but may also be used for workflow status changes or critical errors within an app that should be brought to a user’s attention.

Surface and background colors

  • Surface colors affect surfaces of components, such as cards, sheets, and menus. In standard apps, #FFFFF is used for surfaces. 
  • The background color appears behind scrollable content. The baseline background and surface color is #FAFAFA.

Icon colors

The standard opacity for icons changes based on whether they’re active, focused, or inactive. 

Text-only, non-interactive icons: Use black at 38% on light surfaces; white at 50% on dark surfaces. 

Icon buttons: Use black at 87% for active focused buttons on light; white at 100% for active focused buttons on dark. Use black at 54% for active unfocused buttons on light and white at 70% for active unfocused buttons on light.
