Layout: Grid

The Forge responsive layout grid adapts to screen size and orientation, ensuring consistency across layouts.

Responsive layout grid

Forge layouts are built on a 12 column grid, with columns, gutter, and margin. 

Gutter

Gutters are the spaces between columns. They help separate content.

Use 16px or 24px between columns as standard gutters.

Margin

Margins are the space between content and the left and right edges of the screen.

Margin widths are defined as fixed values at each breakpoint range. To better adapt to the screen, the margin width can change at different breakpoints. Wider margins are more appropriate for larger screens, as they create more whitespace around the perimeter of content.

On a small tablet this layout grid uses 24px margins.

 

On mobile this layout grid uses 16px margins.

Responsive breakpoints

Forge provides responsive layouts based on the following column structures. Layouts using 4-column, 8-column, and 12-column grids are available for use across different screens, devices, and orientations. Each breakpoint range determines the number of columns, and recommended margins and gutters, for each display size.

Content max-width

Many web pages use a max width on content. Placing a max width on your content can be beneficial for the following reasons:

  • Improved readability. An optimal line length of 50 – 75 characters (Baymard Institute, “Typographie”, E. Ruder) facilitates reading on the web by facilitating eye tracking within and between lines; improving focus, and supporting a user’s natural reading rhythm.
  • Improved cognitive fluency. Users actually associate the ease of processing content with the perceived ease of the task (Song, Hyunjin, and Norbert Schwarz, 2008, How Cognitive Fluency Affects Decision Making (UX Matters) ). Using a max width can ease the cognitive load of a page and make it feel easier to interact with.
  • Lower content density. Using a max width can provide focus to the important information by limiting overall content density.

But isn’t white space wasted space?

No. White space can be a great tool to balance design elements, create room to breathe, lower the perceived density of content, feature elements on the page to stand out, and guide the eye to create focus. Overall, designs with more white space are quicker to mentally process and understand, perceived as “less busy,” and are generally more aesthetically pleasing (The power of white space, IDF).

Determining the right max-width for your app

Unfortunately, there is no hard and fast rule for what makes “the perfect” max-width. A good max width for your product will be influenced by the following:

1. Your content. Is your content simple (a form, instructions, stepper, etc) or complex (a dashboard, complex interaction, etc)? Is it text heavy (instructions, forms) or is it content heavy (tables, charts, etc)?

For simple content, use a smaller max width (ie, 960 or 1024px). For complex content, use a larger max width (ie, 1200, 1240, 1360, or even 1440px). For content that’s text heavy, consider line length; we recommend no more  than 75 characters per line. Consider using columns or cards to limit the length of your lines.

2. The 12 column grid. Choosing a max width that’s divisible by 12 will make your life easier. Trust us 🙂

3. Consider your audience. What type of screen will your user be working on? Are they likely to have multiple larger monitors or will they be using a tablet? A smaller desktop or laptop? Google Analytics offers device statistics – use data to better understand what screen sizes your users are likely to use. For apps that are primarily used on multiple large monitors, much wider max widths may be more appropriate, whereas smaller widths may be better for older desktops. For responsive widths, consider setting max widths for specified breakpoints. 

Here are some recommendations for max widths for either simple or complex content. Your content will drive what will work best for your team, but use these as starting points.

The 4px grid

Forge components are built on an 4px grid. Our minimum unit of 4 x 4 pixels drives how column layouts are determined, how elements are designed, and how components interact with each other. 

Grid systems are used to create layouts with a uniform and consistent structure. The goal is to yield predictable flexibility with the least amount of assets and rules.
