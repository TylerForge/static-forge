Forge design system

What is Forge?
Forge is the is the single source of truth which groups all the elements that will allow Tyler teams to design, realize and develop their products. It enables visual and interactive consistency and ensures our applications adhere to design best practices. Forge facilitates efficiency by providing UX guidance, re-usable patterns and components.
How is Tyler Forge different from Google Material Design?
Tyler Forge is a design system rooted in Material Design ‚Äì a visual language that combines principles of good design with technical and scientific innovation. Forge draws from familiar and tested design principles from Google, but allows us to create and modify components and patterns to specific needs of public sector product design. You'll find that our patterns are similiar to Google's, but have been modified to meet the needs of our customer base. A design system that uses shared, reusable components to empower teams to efficiently create applications that express Tyler‚Äôs core values.
What are some of the differences between the legacy and new guidelines?
While Forge builds on legacy TUX guidelines, we've updated design guidance to improvement components for usability concerns or for Tyler's domain specific needs. We'll be publishing those changes on Breaking changes page.
How do I request a new component?
Net new components will need both development work and design guidance. Our team will prefer to modify existing components or create a variation from existing components to meet a team's need, but will create new components as needed. In order for a component to be added to Forge, it should be needed by at least three product teams, otherwise it should be hanlded as a custom component specific to a single team. To request a new component, choose "Request a new component" from our Feedback & support page.
What Slack channels should I join to stay in the know?
Join #tyler-forge for questions and updates to Tyler Forge. We'll use it to announce new content and to answer design and development questions. For general UX information, follow #tyler-ux. In this channel, folks ask UX questions and we announce upcoming UX Clinics, many of which are focused on new Forge content. We'll announce new Forge content in that channel as well.
How do I know what new patterns will be added or what the team is working on next?
The Forge team is continually adding new patterns and components. To get a better idea of what we'll be working on next, check out our Status page. When we add new content or release a new version of the web components, we'll announce it in the #tylerdesignsystem Slack channel as well.

Design

What design tools are open to me if I don't have a Mac?
Tyler supports a symbol library in Figma for PC users. Figma is a web-based collaborative interface design tool that facilitates collaboration and sharable files. Figma can be used from design to prototyping. Check out some guidance to get you started on our Getting started: As a designer page (scroll to the Figma section) or reach out to allison.wolf@tylertech.com for more information.
What design frameworks can be used with Forge components?
Currently, we have a design library for both Sketch and Figma. We're looking into options for Framer X as well.
Accessibility? How do I design for it?
To get started with accessibility check out our Accessibility page. Additionally, you'll find accessibility guidance sections within each our design guidance pages as well as our development pages (coming soon!). In general, developers can use guidance provided in the development guides to add the correct aria attributes for the components. As a designer, add an indication for tab-order if it deviates from the traditional left to right, top to bottom pattern. If anything shoud be skipped by a screenreader, be sure to include a note about that. It may also be helpful to provide guidance about semantic regions of your page.
Can I request design help if I don't have a dedicated UX designer on my team?
Yes! The Tyler UX team offers options different levels of engagement all the way from "Point of Contact" to "Embedded." Check out the "Levels of UX involvement" section on our Getting started with Forge page for more information. Contact kathy.wolf@tylertech.com to request design help.

Does Forge provide guidance for native mobile?
Currently, we have some limited guidance on native mobile. Once Forge is in its MVP state, we'll be looking to expand our native mobile offerings.

For now, check out our Native mobile section on Forge to see our guidance so far. Working on a mobile app? Help us build out Forge by contributing back what you have so far! Email us at tylerforge@tylertech.com to get involved!
What happens if I need to stray from the component design due to a specific application requirement?
In general, Forge seeks to provide guidance around shared patterns over edge or specific cases. Your product may need a component that's very specific to the business case. What we recommend is to bring up the design problem at a UX Clinic (contact david.perk@tylertech.com or post in the #ask-uxchannel). We'll use the UX community to see if a similar component is being used or needed in other products. If it is a shared pattern, we'll work on adding it to Forge. If not, we can provide guidance on UX best practices for your component.
I need to create something in my application quickly but I'm not finding any design guidelines on the Forge site?
If you're looking for a specific pattern or component that doesn't seem to exist on our Forge site, feel free to check out our Status page to see if we may have it coming up, as well as our Backlog to see if we have plans to add it.

Otherwise, follow our guidance for requesting a new component. Alternatively, you can drop a question or suggestion in our #tyler-forge Slack channel. Maybe another team has tackled the same design problem you're thinking about!

Illustrations

How do I know which type of illustration to use?

The illustration system is broken into 3 categories - Spot, Spot Hero and Hero. You can think of those as ‚Äúsmall, medium and large.‚Äù

The smallest, Spot, are small, simple and literal and are meant to be used to add meaning or visual clarity. These work well when placed in cards. These should be used at a minimum size of 40px by 40px and a maximum size of 150px by 150px.

Spot hero illustrations (medium-sized) are more detailed and communicate literal or metaphorical ideas within a limited space. These illustrations are especially good for empty states or error messages. They can also be used within large page headers or larger card card graphics.

Hero illustrations are the largest of the three categories. They are complex and heavy on the narrative, and commonly feature people. They have the most impact at larger sizes, so they are good for full width header images or images that occupy an entire card.

For more detailed information and examples: https://static-forge.netlify.com/foundation/illustrations-usage/

Development

Why isn't my TCW component rendering?

Each custom element HTML tag must be registered with the browser so that it knows how to instantiate the component when it parses the DOM.

Did you remember to define the component(s) when your application is loaded?

There are convenience functions that are exported from the TCW library that will ensure that the elements are properly defined using the customElements API.

See the getting started guide for usage information: Getting Started - Angular

What's the current version of TCW?
The current TCW version is always displayed on the Forge homepage
If I consume the TCW components, what happens when there are upgrades?

The TCW library is available via NPM, and strictly follows semantic versioning. Breaking changes will be limited to major versions. There is a CHANGELOG posted with each release that defines the fixes, features, and breaking changes that pertain to each version.

When installing a new version, if there are no breaking changes, the upgrade path should be seamless.

What frameworks can use TCW?

TCW Web Components are built for the browser platform which means they are framework-agnostic. They integerate seamlessly with all popular JavaScript frameworks including Angular, React, Vue... etc.

For instance, in Angular you can bind to the element properties just as you would any other built-in HTML element.

Who do we reach out to for creating new components?

Please use the Forge feedback & support page for all requests, questions, and general feedback.

Why isn't my binding working in Angular on a TCW component?

This is typically due to binding to the attribute instead of the JavaScript property. For example to set the title text of the omnibar component, you should use this format [titleText]="title" instead of [title-text]="title". Angular uses the JavaScript API of all HTML elements to bind values.

When I click the select I can't see the dropdown?

There could be a few different reasons why the dropdown doesn't show:

  • If you're using template-based tcw-option elements, be sure they are rendered on the page at runtime by inspecting the DOM with your dev console. If there are no options, the dropdown won't display. The same thing goes for if you're using the options property, ensure that it has items in it.
  • When clicking on the select, check your dev console to ensure that the tcw-popup element is being appended to the bottom of the body, as well as ensure that the popup has your expected options within the list. If this is not there, then you likely have a problem with the first issue above, or your select component was defined with the browser.
  • The only other problem here could be a z-index issue. If the tcw-popup element is being placed correctly in the DOM, then try setting the --tyl-z-index-popup CSS custom property to a higher value. This can be applied globally to your application. The z-index ranges are exposed to offer the ability to adjust the floating elements to play nice with other CSS libraries, such as Angular Material.
